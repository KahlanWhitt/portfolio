Affordable Rental Housing Developments in Chicago, Illinois


Executive Summary

   This analysis evaluates the distribution and composition of affordable rental housing across Chicago, using verified city-supported datasets from 2018–2024. The study identifies geographic imbalances, with central regions heavily served and eastern and western regions underserved. Multifamily units dominate the city’s supply, while Senior and ARO units remain limited, highlighting gaps in age-specific and smaller-scale housing. Through descriptive analytics, visualizations, and density mapping, the report provides actionable insights for the City of Chicago to prioritize new developments in underrepresented regions and property types, ensuring equitable access and informed resource allocation. These findings directly support data-driven strategic planning for affordable housing expansion and community development.

Business Problem Definition and Context

   Our business needs to analyze how the number and type of affordable housing units are distributed across different sections of the city. This can help identify imbalances in housing availability; for example, areas with many senior units but few family units. By analyzing data on existing affordable housing developments, including location, property type, and units available, the business can prioritize future projects that fill specific gaps in the local housing market and improve overall community access to affordable housing.


Data Source Assessment

Background on Data Source

   This data-set was originally compiled by the City of Chicago through various housing programs designed to promote and maintain affordability across local neighborhoods. It was later published on the City of Chicago’s open data portal and subsequently accessed through the Open Data Network. The data-set can be viewed directly at: https://www.opendatanetwork.com/dataset/data.cityofchicago.org/s6ha-ppgi


Current Information, Limitations and Further Notes

   The Affordable Rental Housing Developments dataset was compiled by the City of Chicago to document housing projects supported through city programs that promote and maintain affordability. The dataset covers properties located within the municipal boundaries of Chicago and includes information such as property type, address, and number of units. It was first published on July 2, 2018, and most recently updated on December 30, 2024. Updates occur periodically, typically when new projects are completed or when the compliance period for older developments expires, which generally occurs after 30 years. While comprehensive in scope for city-supported developments, the dataset does not include naturally occurring affordable housing or all units available for rent. Users should note that information on rent levels or tenant eligibility is not provided and may require direct contact with property managers.

library(ggplot2)

   Although the graph suggests that numerous affordable housing options exist across Chicago, it also highlights a clear spatial imbalance in their distribution. The concentration of affordable rental developments is noticeably higher in central areas of the city, while availability declines toward both the eastern and western boundaries. This pattern indicates an opportunity to expand affordable housing initiatives beyond the city’s core, increasing accessibility for residents who live or work outside downtown Chicago.

Location Density and Development Opportunity Assessment

housing_clean <- housing_data %>%
filter(!is.na(Longitude),
!is.na(Latitude),
!is.na(Units))
filtered <- housing_clean %>%
  filter(Property_Type %in% c("ARO", "Senior", "Multifamily"))

ggplot(filtered, aes(Longitude, Latitude)) +
  stat_density2d(
    aes(fill = after_stat(level)),
    geom = "polygon",
    alpha = 0.6
  ) +
  scale_fill_gradient(low = "#8edafa", high = "#d84d35") +
  geom_point(aes(size = Units), alpha = 0.5) +
  facet_wrap(~ Property_Type) +
  labs(
    title = "Density of Affordable Housing Units by Property Type",
    subtitle = "Comparing ARO, Senior, and Multifamily Housing Densities Across Chicago",
    x = "Longitude",
    y = "Latitude",
    fill = "Density Level",
    size = "Units"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

   This graph clearly illustrates the distribution of properties across the three main housing programs (ARO, Senior, and Multifamily), making it easy to compare where the city’s development activity is concentrated. By showing which categories have the highest or lowest number of properties, the graph helps decision-makers quickly identify priority areas, evaluate whether resources are aligned with actual demand, and assess whether current policy or investment strategies are producing the intended mix of housing types. Overall, it provides a straightforward, data-driven snapshot of the city’s housing landscape that supports more informed planning and resource allocation.


Strategic Recommendations

Insight 1: Multifamily units dominate all regions, while Senior units are the smallest share.

Insight 2: Regions 1 and 2 have the highest unit concentrations; Regions 3 and 4 are under-served.

Evidence: summary_units table shows Multifamily = ~60–70% of total units per region.


Insight Summary

   Affordable rental housing in Chicago is heavily concentrated in central regions, with far fewer units in eastern and western areas, highlighting opportunities to expand developments in underserved neighborhoods and improve equitable access. Across all regions, Multifamily properties dominate the supply, while Senior housing consistently represents the smallest share, revealing a structural gap in age-specific housing that may leave older residents underserved. Density analysis of ARO, Senior, and Multifamily units further identifies specific locations where certain property types are underrepresented, enabling the city to strategically prioritize future investments. Together, these insights provide a clear, data-driven foundation for guiding decisions on where and what type of affordable housing to develop next, ensuring a more balanced and responsive housing portfolio aligned with community needs.
